## What this series covers

## Lesson 1: The Build — VigilOre

The DRC loses $30 billion a year in the mining sector. Compliance audits take 160 hours per site. I built a multi-agent compliance platform called VigilOre that reduced that to 5 minutes.

This lesson breaks open the system. Five agents, and every one of them is mostly traditional code. The AI is maybe 20 lines per agent. The parser, the scorer, the rate limiter, the branching interview logic — all deterministic. All the 60% and the 30%.

Then it shifts from software to business. How the ratio shaped what I charged ($7,500/month against a $39,000-$59,000 traditional team). How I structured the contract with revenue share and IP protection. Why I could offer month-to-month terms instead of locking the client in.

> The architecture made every one of those decisions for me.

### What you'll walk away with:

How to break down a real AI system into its actual layers, and how those layers directly determine your pricing, contracts, and competitive moat.

### Exercise focus:

Audit your own workflow using the three layers. Find where you're spending time in the wrong one.

## Lesson 2: The Partnership — NLP Logix

NLP Logix is a $31 million *ARR* data science consulting firm. I cold-messaged their CEO on LinkedIn. No deck. No pitch. Two meetings later, we had a full partnership.

This lesson shows how the 60/30/10 stops being about software and starts being about how you structure a business relationship. Five revenue streams from one partnership: retainer, day rates, revenue share, CRO consulting, and content production. Conservative projection over $300,000 a year from roughly 20 hours a month.

The framework mapped onto the deal. The 60% is the contract infrastructure, the invoicing, the course library. The 30% is how each revenue stream feeds the next — training surfaces pain points, pain points become build specs, builds become case studies, case studies become content, content brings the next client. The 10% is the AI that all of it teaches and delivers.

> If you removed the AI and replaced it with any other technology, the structure still works.

### What you'll walk away with:

How to structure multi-stream partnerships where the framework guides what you charge, what you protect, and how each piece compounds.

### Exercise focus:

Map your own professional flywheel. Find where each stage feeds the next and where the loop is leaking.

## Lesson 3: The System Underneath

VigilOre and NLP Logix aren't isolated wins. They're instances of a repeatable model. This lesson reveals the training funnel that sits underneath all of it and shows you the numbers that prove it works.

Three stages. Training goes into a company. Exercises generate structured data about where AI fits, where code fits, where humans stay. That data becomes the spec for custom builds. The client has already seen the framework in action. They understand why you're proposing what you're proposing because they just lived through the exercise that proved it.

600 to 1,000 employees trained across Fortune 500 companies. 95% adoption rate. Industry average is less than 10%. That gap exists because most AI training teaches tools. Tools decay. The 60/30/10 teaches patterns. Patterns survive tool changes.

The lesson closes with my own business mapped to the ratio. Content production, folder architecture, rendering pipelines as the 60%. Content routing, partnership orchestration, training-to-pilot conversion as the 30%. AI calls for generation and analysis as the 10%. One person plus this system replaces what would traditionally require four to six people.

> Same pattern. Same ratio. Different room.

## What you'll walk away with:

The repeatable system behind all of it, the specific numbers that prove adoption, and the framework mapped onto your own work.

## Exercise focus:

Design your own training funnel. Calculate your leverage. Test whether your AI skills are tool skills or pattern skills.
